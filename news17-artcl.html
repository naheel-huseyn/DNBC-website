The Royal Botanic Gardens, Kew, has recently highlighted the digitization of its extensive Fungarium collection, which includes over 1.1 million fungal specimens amassed over 175 years. Among the notable specimens is Gibellula attenboroughii, a parasitic fungus that infects orb-weaving cave spiders, manipulating their behavior before killing them to aid in spore dispersal. This fungus, named in honor of Sir David Attenborough, was first observed in Northern Ireland and is now part of Kew's digital archive, making it accessible to researchers worldwide. The digitization project aims to sequence approximately 7,000 of these fungi, potentially unlocking new compounds and medicines. The collection also includes historical specimens, such as Cyttaria darwinii, collected by Charles Darwin during the HMS Beagle voyage, and Stereum hirsutum, found at 15,000 feet during the 1953 Everest expedition.

This initiative not only preserves the fungal diversity but also provides insights into how species' ranges have shifted over time, aiding in conservation efforts. By georeferencing and sequencing these specimens, scientists hope to tap into the genetic information stored within, accelerating the discovery of new chemicals and medicines. The project underscores the importance of fungi, which have often been overlooked by the scientific community, in addressing challenges like climate change and biodiversity loss. Through this comprehensive digitization, Kew is enhancing global access to its invaluable fungal collections, fostering a deeper understanding of these complex organisms.
